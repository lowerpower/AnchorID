Skeptic FAQ

If AnchorID never becomes popular, it still works.
If it does become popular, it becomes infrastructure.

This page is for people who are unsure whether AnchorID is necessary, trustworthy, or worth the effort. These are reasonable questions. AnchorID is intentionally minimal, and skepticism is expected.

What problem does AnchorID actually solve?

The modern web has no durable way to say "this person is the same person" across time, platforms, and failures.

Usernames change. Domains expire. Companies disappear. Social networks rebrand or collapse.

AnchorID provides a single, stable reference — a UUID-backed URL — that other systems, platforms, and archives can point to without fear that its meaning will drift.

It is not a profile or a social identity. It is infrastructure.

What happens if anchorid.net disappears or is abandoned?

This is expected.

AnchorID is designed under the assumption that the original operator may someday disappear.

If anchorid.net goes offline:

  • Existing UUIDs remain valid identifiers
  • Archived resolver pages continue to exist in caches, archives, and datasets
  • The UUID itself continues to function as a durable identity key

AnchorID leaves behind useful artifacts. A future system does not need AnchorID-specific software — only a URL fetcher and a JSON parser.

Why trust anchorid.net at all? What if the operator alters records?

You should not blindly trust any single operator — including AnchorID.

AnchorID does not provide trust. It provides anchoring.

The system is designed so that:

  • Identity claims are publicly visible
  • External links can be independently verified
  • Changes leave timestamps and audit traces
  • Proofs live on platforms you already control

If an operator behaved maliciously, discrepancies between your AnchorID record and your external proofs would be visible.

The goal is not to eliminate trust, but to make misbehavior detectable.

Why not just use consistent usernames or schema.org sameAs links?

Those approaches work — until the platform changes, rebrands, or disappears.

Usernames are scoped to platforms. sameAs links are references, not anchors.

AnchorID provides a single immutable reference that survives platform churn and migrations. All other identifiers can change while the anchor remains stable.

Isn't this just another identity system?

No.

AnchorID does not:

  • Authenticate users
  • Provide login or SSO
  • Manage permissions
  • Create social graphs
  • Replace existing identity providers

It is a reference point, not a control plane.

Can someone else copy my UUID or impersonate me?

Someone can copy your UUID, just as they can copy your name.

What they cannot do is:

  • Prove control of your website
  • Prove control of your GitHub, LinkedIn, or other profiles
  • Maintain consistent, verifiable claims over time

Identity strength comes from accumulated proofs, not secrecy.

What if I lose my email or access credentials?

AnchorID has no passwords.

Edit access is granted via:

  • One-time email magic links
  • A backup recovery token shown once at creation

If you lose both, there is no recovery path. This is intentional — AnchorID cannot verify you are who you claim to be without these.

Is AnchorID private?

AnchorID is intentionally public.

If you do not want information to be public, do not add it.

The system minimizes private data collection and does not sell or share data, but the identity record itself is meant to be visible, durable, and referenceable.

Who is this actually for?

AnchorID is for people who care about long-term attribution:

  • Independent creators
  • Engineers and researchers
  • Writers, artists, and archivists
  • Systems that ingest and reason over web content

If you do not publish work or care about attribution over decades, AnchorID may not be useful to you.

Why bother doing this now?

Because identity signals compound over time.

Early, stable anchors are more likely to survive future platform shifts, archive pruning, and training data compression.

Creating an AnchorID today is a low-cost signal that may matter later — especially as automated systems increasingly rely on historical continuity.
